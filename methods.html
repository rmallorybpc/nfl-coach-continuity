Methods

How the reunion effect is measured

An event study over player-seasons, with a treatment that requires a real coaching relationship and a control group of like players.

Treatment

A reunion is an event, not a signing

A reunion occurs for a player-season when a player and a coach are on the same team that year, were not both on it the year before, and were together at an earlier team where the coach covered the player's position. The arrival condition is what makes it a reunion rather than a continuation, and it splits the set in two. In a player-move reunion the player arrived; in a coach-move reunion the coach arrived. An earlier signing-based design skipped the arrival condition and counted same-team re-signings as reunions; that error is documented on the audit page.

Coaching relationship

Position-aware, not just same roster

Sharing a roster is not a coaching relationship. A coach counts as having coached a player only if his role covered that player's position group: a head coach covers everyone, a coordinator covers his side of the ball, a position coach covers his own group. This is applied at both ends, the earlier team and the current one, so a defensive coordinator is never credited with a receiver, and it removes the loose matches that manufactured a spurious positive in an earlier cut.

Outcome

Production as a within-position percentile

Each player-season is scored against others at the same position that year. Offense uses PPR fantasy production; defense uses an equal-weight standardized composite of tackles, sacks, tackles for loss, quarterback hits, interceptions, passes defended, and forced fumbles, with a four-game minimum. Availability is the snap-share percentile, which covers the offensive line, where production is not publicly measurable. Percentiles keep positions on one comparable scale and absorb league-wide shifts year to year.

Model and controls

Like compared to like

Each type is compared only to its fair control: player-move reunions against other players who also changed teams, coach-move reunions against other players who also stayed. This matters because moving itself costs production, so comparing reunited movers to everyone would confound the reunion with the move. Every model controls for the player's prior-season percentile, age, position group, and season, and the reunion estimate is read off that. Robustness comes from a permutation placebo, shuffling the reunion label among controls, and from checking that treated and control players are alike on baseline and age.
