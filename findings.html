Results

Key findings

Two facts, and the gap between them is the study. Teams pay a premium to reunite a free agent with a former coach. The production does not repay it.

Finding one

There is no pay premium

On real-market contracts, above two million a year, a player who reunites with a former coach is paid minus 0.8 percent relative to a comparable player, with a 95 percent interval from minus 12 to plus 12 percent (p = 0.90). That is nothing. Teams do not pay up to reunite with a familiar coach.

An earlier cut put the premium at +14 percent. That number lived entirely in the minimum-salary and zero-dollar tier, which is most of the signings, where a small composition difference gets amplified into a spurious figure. On contracts where teams actually make a decision, it disappears.

Finding two

Reunited players retain slightly less

Production does not rise with a reunion. It falls a little. The effect is about six percentile points below zero, and it is consistent whether the reunion runs through the head coach, a coordinator, or a position coach, and whether you look one season out or two.

Availability is roughly flat, a touch negative (−0.02, p = 0.31). Reunion players hold their snap share at about the same rate as everyone else. They play. They just do not produce more with the time, and if anything a little less.

Robustness

Small, but it holds up

The negative is not a selection artifact. Reunion and non-reunion players have identical baseline production and near-identical age, so this is not mean reversion or aging. It survives squared baseline and age terms. And in a placebo test, shuffling the reunion label 200 times centers on zero, while the real effect sits outside the entire placebo range.

It is small, about six percentile points, and it fades to non-significance on market-only or single-side subsamples, so it needs the pooled sample of roughly 74 reunions to see. Read it as a modest drag, not a large one. The full record of how the result changed is in the audit log.

What it means

The belief points the wrong way

The market treats reunions as an edge and talks about them every March. They command no premium once the data is clean, and reunited players retain a little less production, not more. Whatever teams reach for when they bring back a familiar player, it does not show up as pay or as output. The one thing the data will not support is the thing everyone assumes.
