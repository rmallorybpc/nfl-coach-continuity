Key findings

Two ways to reunite, one result

Reunions are split by who moved, and each is compared only to like players. Both come back null on production and on availability.

Every sample size and interval on this page is produced by the committed pipeline. The panel and counts are on the data page; the model, controls, and placebo design are on the methods page.

Finding one

Chasing a former coach buys nothing

A free agent who signs with a team where a former coach now works retains +0.014 more production than a comparable player who also changed teams, on the within-position percentile scale. With a 95 percent interval from −0.015 to +0.044 and p = 0.35, that is a null. Moving costs production for almost everyone; reuniting with a coach does not soften the fall and does not deepen it. This is the well-powered version of the question, 337 reunions against 1,850 other movers (sample detail).

Finding two

Being found by a former coach adds nothing either

A player who stays put and has a former coach arrive on his staff retains +0.014 relative to comparable players who also stayed, interval −0.034 to +0.062, p = 0.57. Also a null. The intuition that a coach who knows you will deploy you better does not show up in the production. It rests on 88 clean coach-arrival reunions, a smaller set than the player-move side, but the estimate is centered in the same place: zero.

Availability

Playing time is flat too

Measured as snap-share percentile, player-move reunions come in at +0.025 (p = 0.13) and coach-move reunions at −0.012 (p = 0.61). Neither is distinguishable from zero. Reuniting does not buy a bigger role any more than it buys production.

Robustness

The null survives a placebo

Shuffling the reunion label at random among the control players, 200 times (how the placebo works), reproduces effects as large as the real ones 34 percent of the time for player-move and 60 percent for coach-move. The real estimates sit comfortably inside the noise. On selection, reunion players are close to their controls on prior production and a touch older (movers 29.2 vs 28.1, stayers 28.8 vs 26.5), and controlling for age and baseline does not move the result.

What it means

The reunion premium the market prices in does not appear in the data. It does not matter whether the player chases the coach or the coach inherits the player; neither raises the production or the playing time the player keeps. The safe reading is not that familiarity is worthless, but that on average it does not move the outcomes a team can measure. If there is an effect, it is smaller than this study, with 8,462 player-seasons behind it, can detect. Anyone paying a premium for a reunion is paying for something the box score does not show.
