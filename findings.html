Results

Key findings

Two facts, and the gap between them is the study. Teams pay a premium to reunite a free agent with a former coach. The production does not repay it.

Finding one

The market pays for continuity

Reunion signings cost more. Holding position, year, and team constant, a player who reunites with a former coach is paid about 14 percent more than a comparable player who does not (p = 0.016), with a 95 percent interval running from roughly plus 3 to plus 27 percent. The deals also run slightly longer, 3.6 years against 3.3.

This is the honest half of the selection problem. Coaches do not sign players they know at random. They pay up for them. So the reunion flag is bound up with the coach's private read on the player, and the premium is the proof of it.

Finding two

The production does not follow

The pay premium does not show up as retained production. The effect is a few percentile points below zero and not distinguishable from it. It does not matter whether the reunion runs through the head coach, a coordinator, or a position coach. It does not matter whether you look one season out or two.

Availability tells the same story from the other side. Reunion players hold their snap share at the same rate as everyone else (p = 0.84). They are not benched and they are not pushed out. They play. They do not produce more with the time.

A correction

The earlier negative did not hold

An earlier cut found a small negative that reached significance. It did not survive a cleaner defensive metric. With an equal-weight standardized metric, the defensive effect goes from −0.08 at p = 0.05 to −0.03 at p = 0.39. Gone. This study claims an absence, not a penalty.

The one place a small negative lingers is skill offense, at −0.08 with p = 0.08, on 24 reunions. A whisper, not a result.

What it means

Premium without payoff

Teams pay for coach continuity. The production does not repay it. That gap answers the question the design could not settle on its own. We could never fully separate whether continuity helps the player or whether coaches simply know who will transfer. Neither shows up as production. Whatever coaches buy when they reunite with a player, it is not measurable output on the field.
